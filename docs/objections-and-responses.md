# Objections and Responses

> **Source:** 12 discovery-call transcripts recorded by Michael Kodinsky on Fathom, Dec 2025–May 2026. Every prospect quote and every Mike response below is verbatim from a real call.

Across these calls, Mike's response pattern to almost every objection is the same: **(1) acknowledge it explicitly, (2) reframe it using a different time horizon or a different comparison, (3) name the tradeoff honestly, (4) give the prospect the dignity to walk away.** He never argues, never pressures, and several times closes himself out of a deal mid-call rather than push.

The objections below are the ones that actually came up. Some objections the prompt anticipated (e.g., "I want to think about it," "I want to go direct to a lender") didn't appear cleanly in the sample — flagged in the gaps section at the end.

---

## 16. "I was hoping YOU were the capital provider" / "Why pay a middleman?"

The go-direct objection, now with a verbatim instance (Doug Arthur, a banker himself, expected Mike to be the lender).

**The value argument for not going direct (Stephen Deason call, [25:45](https://fathom.video/calls/671917601?timestamp=1545)):**
> *"on-deck as an example, you go to them directly… they'll put as much points and fee… as they think they can get away with… When we bring a deal to them, we know how to work with them, and so we're going to get the best possible structure. That's how we more than cover our own fee."*

**Underneath it:** they think the advisor is a tax. Mike's answer is that the advisor *more than pays for himself* by getting structure/pricing a direct borrower can't, and by running a competitive process. He never argues — he invites them to go call around, confident they'll come back.

## 17. "Just send me a few lender options first, then I'll decide if I'll engage"

The reverse-engagement objection — prospect wants names/quotes before signing. Mike's move: get *soft quotes* to prove value, paired with a small DD fee so there's skin in the game, and structure the engagement in steps.

**Designer Eyes / Amit — owner is success-fee-oriented, won't commit upfront, [30:20](https://fathom.video/calls/588010625?timestamp=1820):**

**Mike's phased response — soft quotes first, then a meeting, then engage, [18:00](https://fathom.video/calls/588010625?timestamp=1080):**
> *"I'm willing to go take this to some of our real estate lenders. Get some soft quotes, what it's going to look like, and then as a next step, I would ask that we could have a meeting with you and [the owner]… earn a small DD fee… because I want to be able to say [we] can do it in steps."*

## How the bot should use this

- **Always acknowledge first, reframe second.** Mike never argues. He says "I hear you" or "that's a fair concern" before he reframes.
- **Be willing to lose the deal.** Mike walks away from deals openly, repeatedly. The bot should too. A walkaway is not a failure — it's part of the brand.
- **Never beat a bank rate.** If a prospect has a real bank offer cheaper than what Mike could deliver, the answer is to say "take that" and offer to stay engaged for the next deal.
- **Show the math live.** When the objection is about fees or rate, do the math with the prospect's actual numbers. Don't say "our fee is 2%" — say "on a $1M line, that's $20K, of which $5K is the upfront DD that comes off the success fee."
- **Distress mode requires extra honesty.** When a prospect is in crisis, the bot should err toward telling them what won't work, not what might. Mike's pattern is to walk distressed prospects through what they're really asking for ("you're about to take another MCA, behind six others, even worse than the last one") so they can hear themselves.
