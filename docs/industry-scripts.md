# Industry Scripts — How Mike Tailors the Pitch by Vertical

> **Source:** Fathom discovery calls recorded by Michael Kodinsky, Dec 2025–May 2026 (original 12 + a second pass of ~20 calls mined to fill industry gaps). Every quote is verbatim with a Fathom timestamp link. This file covers how Mike's framing, product default, and qualifying questions *shift by industry* — the stuff a generic playbook misses.

Mike is a generalist by design, but he reads each industry's cash-flow physics differently. The bot should detect the prospect's industry early and pull the matching section here. Each section gives: **the typical situation → Mike's default product → industry-specific framing (verbatim) → the questions Mike asks this vertical → what to watch for.**

---

## 1. Construction & Contractors

**Coverage: STRONG** (Doug Arthur, Sal Cataldo / Construction Unlimited, Nick Scaff)

### Typical situation
Profitable-but-cash-strapped GC or sub. The wall is almost always **timing**: progress billing + retainage + a shock event (lost contract, slow customer, owner draw) that triggered an MCA stack. Construction AR is *thin and lumpy* relative to revenue — few customers, milestone billing — which makes pure factoring harder than people expect.

### Mike's default product
Rarely clean factoring. More often: **MCA consolidation into a true-term or revenue-based product**, an **AR line where the receivables support it** (often subordinating an SBA, which usually allows it), a **real-estate-backed bridge** on owned land, or **job-mobilization financing** to cover upfront materials/labor.

### Industry-specific framing (verbatim)

**Retainage is Mike's #1 construction probe — he asks for the tail directly:**
> *"What's the tail on that, on retainage on average? Six months, ten months, eight months, what do you—"* — Mike to Sal Cataldo, [11:02](https://fathom.video/calls/570164710?timestamp=662)

> Sal: *"Some of it could be six to eight months, sometimes it's a year. Because we're on the front side of the construction cycle, because we put the roof on, and then they've got to finish everything."* — [11:05](https://fathom.video/calls/570164710?timestamp=665)

**Progress billing + carry burden — even a profitable job hits a cash wall (Doug Arthur / Tesla):**
> Doug: *"With Tesla, 25% when they sign the PO and step on the site, they bill, it's 30-day terms, and then when they're completed, they bill the final, and Tesla pays like clockwork in 30 days… if there are issues, and it becomes a 35-day bill, now you've got, like, a whole extra payroll in there… the margins are sufficient that it can handle it, it's just there's a cash shortage."* — [Doug Arthur call](https://fathom.video/calls/543844168)

**The shock event that starts the MCA stack:**
> Doug: *"The whole problem started back in late April of 24… Elon Musk laid off the entire supercharging division by, via email at 2:15 in the morning. And they owed us a million four at the time, had a significant payroll to make on Thursday."* — [Doug Arthur call](https://fathom.video/calls/543844168)

**B2B vs B2C margin split — why a pivot changes the capital need (Sal):**
> Sal: *"The gross profit is now at about 15% to 18% on that B2B side in just builder homes… in our GC work, we make 20% to 25%… And then on those B2C, where you're dealing directly with the homeowner, that's where your margins shoot way up into the 40s."* — [08:20](https://fathom.video/calls/570164710?timestamp=500) / [11:24](https://fathom.video/calls/570164710?timestamp=684)

**SBA usually subordinates to a new AR line — Mike's standard read:**
> *"It's a rare case that a 7A loan holder would disagree to subordinate for AR specifically. It's pretty common. So we'll assume the best there."* — Sal Cataldo call, [~12:00](https://fathom.video/calls/570164710?timestamp=720)

**Job-mobilization financing exists for construction (covers upfront job costs):**
> *"we have a group that will essentially do financing to mobilize jobs… where they cover the upfront costs of materials, labor, you know, for projects and jobs."* — Doug Arthur call

**Why factoring is hard in construction (implicit, never preached):** thin AR, 3–4 customers, job-based billing, retainage holdbacks. Mike pivots to consolidation/term/RE-bridge instead of forcing an AR line.

### Questions Mike asks construction prospects
- *"Do they invoice on terms or do they get paid upon completion?"*
- *"What's the tail on that, on retainage on average?"*
- *"How much of your equipment… is under some kind of a lease or a loan, how much of it's free and clear?"*
- *"How much do you carry in accounts receivable on average?"* (expecting it to be thin relative to revenue)
- *"When did you take [the MCA] out? Are we [in] the last six months or so?"* / *"How many positions?"*

### What to watch for
MCA stacking is the chronic disease. Mike treats it as a **cash-flow design problem, not a character flaw** — *"they're like a drug, these MCAs… he keeps coming back to the well."* The job: get them one good consolidation step up the ladder, then keep them off the well.

## 2. Staffing Agencies

**Coverage: GOOD** (Alan A. / $13M staffing agency)

### Typical situation
Fast-growing staffing company with **weekly/bi-weekly payroll** but 30–60 day client receivables. Often declined by a bank on the **owner's personal credit** — which is exactly the wrong lens, because the right lens is the *account debtor's* credit.

### Mike's default product
**AR-based revolving line (factoring or ABL).** Staffing is the textbook fit — quality recurring receivables (hospitality, commercial, GovCon obligors) against a relentless payroll obligation. A **fast revenue-based line** can bridge while the AR facility is set up.

### Industry-specific framing (verbatim)

**The killer insight — asset-based lenders don't gate on owner credit:**
> *"The asset-based owners don't weigh personal credit all that heavily… They look at the credit of the account debtors. That's what they care about is, okay, you're invoicing ABC Company or the U.S. Government… We call them account debtors in that vernacular."* — Alan A. call

**Sizing a staffing AR line on the fly:**
> *"Receivables, on average — if they're doing $13 million annually… it could be as much as $2 million, it could be as little as half a million, it just, it all depends."* — Alan A. call

**GovCon mix drives lender routing (staffing often has a government slice):**
> *"What percentage of their sales or business is GovCon, actually government contracts versus just non-GovCon?"* — Alan A. call. (80% commercial / 20% GovCon → generalist AR lender; 80–100% GovCon → specialist like "Reign.")

**Payroll-bridge speed is the variable — the fast option:**
> *"We also have a revenue-based line of credit lender… They can move and close and fund within days, literally like under a week… true APR rate in the mid-teens… capped at $350,000… we have other lenders that can go to up to $750,000."* — Alan A. call

**Protect the referring banker — build in a no-penalty exit if they bank-repatriate:**
> *"if it's the referring bank… we'll write it in so that if they go and move their business to the referring bank… the lender would waive any kind of prepayment penalty."* — Alan A. call

### Questions Mike asks staffing prospects
- *"What percentage of their sales or business is GovCon versus non-GovCon?"*
- *"Receivables, on average, do you know?"*
- *"The main hurdle for the bank was the personal credit of the ownership, is that right?"*
- *"How much do they need access to — they were looking for a half a million line?"*

### What to watch for
Don't over-size the line. *"don't take a bigger line size than you need now, knowing that once you get approved, you can raise it at a moment."* And lead with the credit reframe — it's the single thing that turns a bank "no" into a "yes."

## 3. Government Contractors

**Coverage: STRONG** (Stan Ciepcielinski / Helicon, Von / Mal Tech Fleet, Oliver Weilandt / DSNY)

### Typical situation
Won a big government contract → now has to **fund production up front** because the government refuses dealer/intermediary financing, then waits 30–60+ days from *acceptance* (not delivery) to get paid. Milestone billing creates peaks and valleys.

### Mike's default product
**PO financing + AR financing under one roof** (PO funds production, AR takes out the PO at invoice). Sometimes a **spot/revenue-based bridge** for sporadic billing. For 100% GovCon shops, a **GovCon-specialist lender.**

### Industry-specific framing (verbatim)

**The core GovCon pattern — no dealer, self-fund, 60 days from acceptance (DSNY):**
> Oliver: *"The city of New York doesn't like that, they want to have a direct contract with the OEM, so there's no dealer flooring, so… we're just funding it on our own, and then… it takes them about 60 days from the time that they accept the vehicle… then 60 days later, they pay us."* — [00:51](https://fathom.video/calls/539473125?timestamp=51)

**PO money costs more than AR money — and *why* (Mike's economics):**
> *"PO financing, the time, the float time of the money is more expensive than AR financing, because… AR is obviously closer to liquidity, so therefore it's less risk and it comes out half the price."* — Oliver Weilandt call, [06:34](https://fathom.video/calls/539473125?timestamp=394)

**Annualized GovCon PO+AR pricing — confirmed live:**
> Oliver: *"So, if we annualize it, we're talking mid-teens on the AR and mid-20s on the PO side?"* / Mike: *"…below the mid-teens on the AR and mid-20s on the PO, exactly. You got it."* — [09:18](https://fathom.video/calls/539473125?timestamp=558)

**Assignment of claims + CO sign-off slows gov AR — so set up a facility, not spot (Helicon):**
> *"with government, it may take a little longer… you've got to get the assignment of claims, you've got to get the CO to sign off. So… it's probably better to set up a true facility… they may build in some nominal costs, like… an unused line fee of 50 basis points."* — Stan Ciepcielinski call, [14:02](https://fathom.video/calls/509683042?timestamp=842)

**100% GovCon → use a specialist:**
> *"There are lots of AR financing companies that will do government contracts… And then there's several that it's like 100% of all they do. So if 100% of what this company does… is GovCon work, then… your best bet is to go with a company that specializes in that."* — Stan Ciepcielinski call, [09:37](https://fathom.video/calls/509683042?timestamp=577)

**Gov slow-pay is normal, not alarming:**
> Von: *"for the government entities, it roughly takes between 30 to 45 days to pay us… on a net 30, they may pay up to 45."* / Mike: *"it's not — not atypical at all for lots of entities, much in government included."* — Mal Tech Fleet call, [09:39](https://fathom.video/calls/528358910?timestamp=579)

**Staggered delivery + the 120-day capital commitment (DSNY):**
> Oliver: *"60 days PO plus 60 days AR. You could look [at] 120 days PO plus 60 days AR."* — [03:30](https://fathom.video/calls/539473125?timestamp=210)

### Questions Mike asks GovCon prospects
- *"So, are we talking about Accounts Receivable Financing, Purchase Order Financing, or both?"*
- *"What's that float time typically like?"* (PO build cycle)
- *"How much of your volume is going to be with [the gov account] over the course of a 12-month span?"*
- *"Is there existing invoices?"* (PO vs. AR test — asked before assuming a product)
- *"Was that [DoD money] essentially a grant or an investment equity or what?"* (understanding SBIR/contract funding)

### What to watch for
Government receivables are **low credit risk but slow + procedurally heavy**. The unused-line-fee structure solves sporadic billing. Always confirm whether they need PO (pre-invoice) or AR (post-invoice) money — Mike asks this *first*.

## 4. E-Commerce / DTC

**Coverage: STRONG** (Designer Eyes / Amit, plus Blinc from the original 12)

### Typical situation
$10–40M consumer e-commerce brand (Amazon/DTC) that's inventory-hungry and seasonal — but has **no B2B receivables to factor** because it sells to consumers. Owner is often rate-sensitive and balks at inventory/ABL pricing.

### Mike's default product
When the owner won't accept 15–25%+ inventory/ABL rates: **real-estate cash-out** (if they own property) to fund inventory cheaply. Otherwise **inventory financing** (pays your vendors) or **e-comm-specialist ABL** for those who'll accept the rate. Factoring is structurally off the table.

### Industry-specific framing (verbatim)

**Why ABL/factoring doesn't fit a rate-sensitive e-comm owner (Designer Eyes):**
> Amit: *"I am not even suggesting asset-based lending, because obviously, as soon as he starts to see the number, it's just going to be a no deal. So, I'm not going to spend my time, because we spent a lot of time last year…"* — [20:11](https://fathom.video/calls/588010625?timestamp=1211)

**The workaround — RE cash-out as the cheap capital for inventory:**
> Amit (relaying Mike's coaching): *"the cleanest deal, in my opinion, will be something which is real estate-backed, it's fully paid off… an asset-based deal or like inventory-based lending is going to be expensive."* — [06:37](https://fathom.video/calls/629451527?timestamp=397)

**Timing — pull capital in the slow season to pre-fund peak inventory:**
> Amit: *"if you're going to take on debt, this is the time you need most because by the time you get to the summer months, the revenue picks up again… if you think about the Amazon business, we need to give them the inventory a month or two months in advance before the holiday season… it pays to have the inventory on hand and the cash on hand so you can stock up."* — [11:19](https://fathom.video/calls/588010625?timestamp=679)

**The e-comm inventory specialist (from the Blinc call, original 12) — exists but needs a senior lien:**
> *"one of our asset-based lenders has a standalone inventory financing program… but only for e-commerce inventory… prime plus two kind of rate, revolving line type of structure. But again, they need to have a senior on inventory."* — Blinc call, [46:21](https://fathom.video/calls/672510528?timestamp=2781)

### Questions Mike asks e-comm prospects
- *"You mentioned that you have, like, 40% of it is through Amazon… do you guys have any inventory housed at an Amazon warehouse?"*
- *"What would be the use of funds?"* (almost always inventory + slow-season bridge)
- *"Did you end up wiping all the debt… is the company debt-free right now?"*
- *"Do you have real estate… free and clear or with equity behind it?"* (the pivot to cheap capital)

### What to watch for
The trap is wasting cycles re-pitching ABL to a rate-sensitive owner who already said no. **Pattern recognition: e-comm + rate-sensitive owner → stop at ABL, go straight to real estate.** If there's no property, inventory financing or an e-comm ABL is the realistic (pricier) path — set that expectation honestly.

## 5. Healthcare & Medical Practices

**Coverage: STRONG** (Maria Rojas / PET Scan, Daryl Wakefield / dental from original 12, CareConnect, Josh Harper, Stephen Deason / Opa from original 12)

### Typical situation
Two very different sub-types:
- **Practices that bill insurance** (imaging, dental, specialty) → strong but slow receivables (Medicare/Humana/Blue Cross pay 30–90 days), often a credentialing or accreditation gap creating a cash void.
- **Asset-light services** (home care, home health) → little to no factorable AR; mostly private-pay or VA; the only path is revenue-based.

### Mike's default product
**Medical A/R factoring** (specialized — only ~10–15 of ~700 US factors do medical) for insurance-billing practices. **Revenue-based line** for asset-light home care. **SaaS/recurring-revenue debt** for healthcare tech.

### Industry-specific framing (verbatim)

**The triangle analogy for medical factoring (Maria Rojas):**
> *"You're not making any payments at all… Because what you're doing is you're selling them your receivable… It's like a triangle. It's like a three-part relationship. You, the vendor, and your customer, which is Humana, Medicare, whoever else."* — [1:13:21](https://fathom.video/calls/592239574?timestamp=4401)

**Worked example — Humana 30-day vs Blue Cross 60-day:**
> *"you invoice Humana $1,000… 90% advance rate… you can borrow $900… In 30 days, Humana pays… their fees are 2% every month… 2% of $1,000… $20."* / *"In the case of Blue Cross… in 60 days… their fee is now 4%, not 2%, so instead of keeping $20, they're actually keeping $40."* — [1:11:23](https://fathom.video/calls/592239574?timestamp=4283) / [1:16:35](https://fathom.video/calls/592239574?timestamp=4595)

**Medical factoring is specialized — DACA accounts, lower advance (Daryl, original 12):**
> *"there's probably 700 factors in the US and maybe 10 or 15 of them do medical… insurances won't do what's called an assignment… they set up typically what's called a DACA account… you'll have an advanced rate of, could be 65, 70%. It's on the lower side of medical because… you get paid oftentimes 50 cents on the dollar."* — Daryl call, [00:04](https://fathom.video/calls/641678904?timestamp=4)

**The credentialing/accreditation cash void (imaging-specific):**
> Maria: *"we normally need to process with the images, send it to do ACR, and then after ACR, I will get paid… it will take, after we start, at least three months."* — [40:06](https://fathom.video/calls/592239574?timestamp=2406)

**Asset-light home care → revenue-based only, gated on consistent deposits (Josh Harper):**
> *"People are great assets, but not the kind that you borrow."* — [15:00](https://fathom.video/calls/520361175?timestamp=900)
> *"The revenue-based lenders… won't look at the loss… if you can show a three-month run of enough revenue… The only thing that would really hurt your chances… would be if December was really low… because that tells a story that they don't want to see."* — [18:21](https://fathom.video/calls/520361175?timestamp=1101) / [19:02](https://fathom.video/calls/520361175?timestamp=1142)

**D2C home care can't factor (Stephen Deason / Opa, original 12):**
> *"the challenge is you can't [factor] because it's not, you don't have commercial B2B AR."* — Stephen Deason call, [14:25](https://fathom.video/calls/671917601?timestamp=865)

**Mike's compassionate read on medical operators:**
> *"In the medical space… doctors and medical people who run medical type businesses are not usually as aware of their business figures as maybe people in other industries, because it already takes so much from you to be a doctor."* — [1:03:35](https://fathom.video/calls/592239574?timestamp=3815)

### Questions Mike asks healthcare prospects
- *"Is your model… insurance pay, or is it all self-pay, private pay?"* (the factor-or-not fork)
- *"When you finish a scan and you invoice an insurance company… how long before they pay on average?"*
- *"What was the reason [the banker] said you didn't qualify for the bank financing?"*
- *"Do you have any equipment that's free and clear… or it all has a loan?"* (equipment + WC combos)

### What to watch for
Insurance-billing practices with sizeable AR → medical factoring beats a revenue-based loan. Asset-light care → revenue-based, and the **3-month consistent-deposit history** is the gate. Adjust explanation depth — medical founders are often financially unsophisticated; reframe compassionately, never condescendingly.

## 6. Manufacturers

**Coverage: STRONG** (Acela / trucks, Lowe & Fletcher from original 12, DANNIK / medical devices)

### Typical situation
Long production cycle (weeks to 9 months), inputs from many vendors, customers slow to pay or no longer paying deposits. The cash gap sits in **work-in-process** before there's an invoice to factor.

### Mike's default product
**ABL** is the right *permanent* structure (borrows against WIP + finished inventory + AR + equipment) but takes 6–8 weeks. A **revenue-based bridge** is "step one" to stabilize fast. **WIP financing** when there's no clean PO-to-finished-good (custom manufacturers). Straight **PO financing fails** for custom manufacturers with fragmented vendor inputs.

### Industry-specific framing (verbatim)

**Why PO financing doesn't work for custom manufacturers (Acela):**
> Stan: *"the traditional PO cycle won't work. PO financing won't work because… they buy a cable… they have to rewire everything. They have to buy stoplights… seats… radios… everything comes from a million different sources."* — [19:23](https://fathom.video/calls/579188462?timestamp=1163)

**Mike's "one-then-three" sequencing — bridge first, ABL second:**
> *"let's get this bridge in place because it can be quick… and then knowing that the ABL structure is going to take six to eight weeks to get in place anyway, I started… a one, three approach, and we started with one which is this kind of revenue-based approach for quick funding."* — Acela call, [28:12](https://fathom.video/calls/579188462?timestamp=1692)

**The deposit-cycle collapse (the trigger):**
> Stan: *"they're sitting with a lot of deposits… and now the deposit cycles have ceased… 85% to 90% is city council. So, there is going to be no deposits… Either they have to find alternative financing or this model doesn't work."* — [20:47](https://fathom.video/calls/579188462?timestamp=1247) / [22:11](https://fathom.video/calls/579188462?timestamp=1331)

**GAAP installment accounting makes bank-statement underwriting hard:**
> Stan: *"under the gap accounting… you can recognize the gross profit percentage… you'll never ever do a cash to income reconciliation. It'll never work."* — [04:29](https://fathom.video/calls/579188462?timestamp=269)

**WIP financing for component-buying manufacturers (DANNIK):**
> Gary: *"we are a medical device manufacturing company, so we buy all the little widgets that go into making a finished good. I do not cut a purchase order to some company that just makes the finished good… people find it complex."* — [22:50](https://fathom.video/calls/522862734?timestamp=1370)
> Build cycle + receivable cycle = up to 150–180 days. *"60 to 90-day build cycle, and then a 60-day receivable cycle."* — [08:19](https://fathom.video/calls/522862734?timestamp=499)

**Factoring/ABL for a manufacturer with blue-chip customers (Lowe & Fletcher, original 12):**
> *"yours is, your bread and butter, manufacturing, you sell to blue chip customers, these are things they like, so you would have a strong profile in their eyes."* — Lowe & Fletcher call, [~28:00](https://fathom.video/calls/659542194?timestamp=1680)

### Questions Mike asks manufacturer prospects
- *"What's your open AR look like typically?"* and *"how much of that [inventory] is customer specific versus not?"*
- *"Are you recognizing revenue at the point of installation?"* (cycle/accounting probe)
- Use-of-funds precision: *"Stan, speak to the use of funds in a precise way"* — chassis, engines, vendor payments, auction buys.

### What to watch for
Custom manufacturers = WIP/ABL, **not** PO financing. Municipal/blue-chip customer concentration is *low credit risk but deposit/timing risk*. Lead with the bridge to stabilize, then assemble the ABL. The business quality usually isn't the question — the liquidity gap is.

## 7. Professional Services (legal, accounting, consulting)

**Coverage: THIN** — only partial signal (Eli / TheBestNotary, legal-tech, from the original 12). **This vertical needs a dedicated Mike recording** — see `recording-script-for-mike.md`.

### What we have
The closest material is legal-*tech* (a B2B SaaS-ish fulfillment platform for attorneys), not a pure professional-services firm. From the Eli call: cash-flow-based businesses with no hard assets are underwritten on revenue, and MCA consolidation for stacked services businesses is "difficult by nature."
> *"yours is a cash flow based business… it's not assets specific to the business, that can be monetized or that could be valued per se."* — Eli call, [29:33](https://fathom.video/calls/631676745?timestamp=1773)

### What to watch for
Until Mike records the canonical professional-services pitch, the bot should treat law/accounting/consulting firms as **cash-flow-underwritten** (revenue-based line, possibly AR line if they bill business clients on terms) and **escalate to a human** rather than improvising an industry-specific script that isn't grounded in a transcript.

## How the bot should use this

- **Detect the industry early** and pull the matching section before recommending a product. The same revenue number means different things in construction (lumpy AR) vs. staffing (clean recurring AR) vs. e-comm (no factorable AR).
- **Lead with the industry's cash-flow physics, not the product.** Mike never opens with "you need factoring" — he opens with "tell me how you get paid," then the product reveals itself.
- **Use the verbatim reframes.** The staffing credit reframe, the GovCon "PO costs more because it's further from liquidity," the construction retainage probe — these are the lines that build trust because they prove Mike *knows the industry*.
- **Respect the thin verticals.** Professional services is not well-covered. Don't fabricate an industry script — say what's grounded and route to a human for the rest.
