# Product Explanations

> **Source:** 12 discovery-call transcripts recorded by Michael Kodinsky on Fathom, Dec 2025–May 2026. Each explanation below is Mike's verbatim plain-English explanation, captured from a live call. These are the explanations a non-finance prospect actually understood — preserve the analogies and the rhythm.

Mike's pattern when explaining any product: **(1) name it → (2) give a simple analogy → (3) walk through the mechanics with the prospect's actual numbers → (4) check in with "does that make sense?"**

## 1. AR Factoring — the gold-standard explanation

Mike's clearest, most fluent product explanation. He uses three analogies stacked: **(1) a triangle (you + customer + factor), (2) water in a cup (the line replenishes), and (3) a lockbox (separate bank account).** This is the most repeated product in his book.

**Lwany Sarabia call, [05:24](https://fathom.video/calls/522844430?timestamp=324):**

> *"Invoice Financing is probably your best tool for what you're describing… Invoice Financing, there's two types. Invoice They're almost the same. They're like close cousins. But basically, the idea is in one of them, one of them is a loan product where it shows up on your balance sheet as debt. The other one is not. And I'm going to talk about that other one because I think it's probably better for you. It's actually not a debt product. It's a recurring sale of an asset. Let me explain what I mean by that. Your receivable is an asset. Your receivable You've completed the work and you've invoiced the client and the customer and their promise, according to the agreement, is that they'll pay you in 60 days… What an invoice financing company will do is they'll step in, acting like a third party, almost like a triangle, you, the customer, and them. And they will buy that receivable from you at a slight discount. In other words… They'll advance you on it, not 100 cents on the dollar, but maybe 80, 85, maybe a little lower, maybe a little higher. Let's just say 80 cents on the dollar, just to be safe."*

He continues with mechanics ([Lwany, ~06:00](https://fathom.video/calls/522844430?timestamp=360)):

> *"you'll have a portal. You'll set up, it'll be a revolving line of credit, basically. Okay. But it will be backed by your receivables. So the line may be set at some amount that makes sense to you. Maybe let's say a million dollars… And so you can basically, so you'll have a portal. you'll, basically, you upload your receivables to their portal. They'll make sure there's nothing that's ineligible. Ineligible would be a case where it becomes more than 60 days past due."*

The water-in-a-cup metaphor ([Lwany, ~09:00](https://fathom.video/calls/522844430?timestamp=540)):

> *"the lender sets up what's called a lockbox. It's like a different bank account that the payments will go to that account. So you would inform your customers, look, starting on January the 23rd or whenever…please remit payments over here to this Bank of America account… So it's not like you're making payments to the line. The payments come right into them and it immediately pays down… more availability is, the availability rises for you. It's almost like water in a cup, you know. It's like as, as they pay down the line, it fills up again and you can borrow down again as you need to."*

Worked example ([Lwany, ~10:30](https://fathom.video/calls/522844430?timestamp=630)):

> *"if you think of it like in simple terms, let's say you only had one customer and you invoiced them one invoice, and you uploaded it to their portal, and it was a million dollars… Let's say you had an 80% advance rate, so you can borrow, you know, $800,000 against it, and they don't pay it for 90 days, when that payment comes in, that last, the remaining 20% that was held back, it's like a reserve that they keep, will then be remitted to you, less their fees, and their fees would be, in my example, if we're assuming about 1.5% a month, and it's been 90 days, they're going to take 4.5% out in that scenario. So you're going to end up keeping 95.5% or, you know, or $950,000."*

Then check-in: *"Does that make sense, just as a general mechanics?"* — [Lwany, 11:44](https://fathom.video/calls/522844430?timestamp=704).

## How to choose the product — Mike's working logic

Across all 12 transcripts, Mike's product-fit decision tree looks like this:

1. **Does the business have commercial B2B receivables?** → Factoring or ABL (his default). For Lwany ($3M revenue, 60-day Encore terms) → factoring. For Chuck Wahr (Lowe & Fletcher, blue-chip OEM customers) → factoring or ABL. *Not viable* for Stephen Deason (D2C home care) or Schuyler (Nanny Network has only $42k AR average — too small).
2. **Does the business have inventory at cost on the balance sheet?** → Standalone inventory lender or inventory-backed line. For Carlos (Venicasa, claimed $2M inventory at cost) → potential inventory + PO + AR stack.
3. **Does the business have real estate (free-and-clear or with equity)?** → Real estate as cheapest capital source.
4. **Does the business have an existing PO that needs production financing?** → PO + AR combo.
5. **Acquisition with a profitable seller?** → SBA or non-SBA 10-year term loan (no home lien).
6. **None of the above, only cash flow / revenue history?** → Revenue-based line or term. Sized at ~10-15% of annual revenue.
7. **MCA-stacked, no assets, no equity to leverage?** → Article 9 (last resort) or honest walkaway.

## How the bot should use this

- **Never deliver a product explanation without an analogy.** Mike's pattern is mechanic + analogy + worked example. Strip out either of the first two and it sounds like a textbook.
- **Always use the prospect's actual numbers.** Mike never uses round numbers — he plugs in the prospect's own revenue, AR balance, etc. The bot should ask for those numbers before explaining a product if it doesn't have them.
- **Check in.** End any product explanation with "Does that make sense?" — Mike does this multiple times per minute when explaining.
- **Pair products, don't isolate.** Mike's biggest moves are multi-prong (PO + AR; factoring + RBF subordinate; inventory + revenue-based). The bot should default to "here's a layered approach" rather than "here's the one product for you."
- **Be willing to say "this isn't a product I do."** SBA, equity, leasing, etc. — Mike refers these out. The bot should too.
