## Cross-Call Summary Notes for Gap Editors

### Gap #10 — Bridge / Unsecured Term: When and WHY over secured options

**Strong calls:** Acela (Call 5), Josh Waddell (Call 1)
**Weak calls:** Doug Van Poppel (Call 2 — bridge fails due to absence of cash-flowing business; useful as counter-example)

**Pattern emerging across calls:**
- Mike deploys bridge/RBF as the "fast step one" when: (a) there's an ABL/RE solution coming but takes 6–8 weeks, (b) a company has recurring revenue or strong PO backlog, (c) traditional venture/bank debt has runway or size gating requirements the company can't meet yet.
- Bridge is NOT recommended when the repayment source is "soft-committed investors" or speculative asset appreciation (Van Poppel counter-example).
- Mike's sequencing language: "one, three approach" — bridge first, ABL second.
- Key Mike distinction vs. fixed-fee RBF: line-of-credit-style prepayment flexibility.

**Mike on why bridge even when it costs more:**
The Josh Waddell call shows the implicit logic: Leiter required 12 months runway; Josh had 3–4. The bridge fills the gap to get the company to a state where cheaper, larger capital is available. Mike doesn't preach this explicitly in this call — he simply recognizes the situation and tries to find the best-cost bridge available. The anti-pattern is overselling: Mike explicitly says "I'm not going to blow smoke."

### Gap #11 — Real Estate Cash-Out for Working Capital

**Strong calls:** Call 3 (Amit pre-call), Call 4 (Designer Eyes lender intake), Call 2 (Van Poppel, RE mechanics as counter-example)
**Weak calls:** Josh Waddell (no RE involved)

**Key patterns:**
- RE cash-out is recommended when: owner is rate-sensitive (wants single digits), business cash flows are negative/unqualified for ABL, and there is a free-and-clear or lightly encumbered property.
- Mike consistently frames RE as "cheapest capital" for the right profile — not in those words, but by contrasting it to ABL rates.
- Land (unimproved) = 50% LTV maximum; improved commercial property can support 65%+ LTV.
- PROPCO/OPCO structures are common; lenders underwrite both.
- SBA is a viable path for businesses with historical losses if they have real estate and a defensible proforma.
- Sell-leaseback is raised as an alternative when owner wants to maximize cash extraction and is willing to sacrifice the asset.
- Timing insight: best to pull RE capital during slow season to pre-fund inventory before peak.

### Gap #18 — E-Commerce / DTC: When Factoring Does NOT Fit

**Strong calls:** Call 3 and Call 4 (Designer Eyes — Amit and Mike both confirm ABL/factoring is off the table for consumer e-comm; RE is the answer)

The RE-backed path is the workaround — use the property to generate cheap working capital to fund inventory. **Anti-pattern:** Mike and Amit have learned from a prior failed attempt at ABL/inventory lending for Designer Eyes. They don't re-run it. Pattern recognition: when the owner is rate-sensitive and the business is e-commerce, stop at ABL and go straight to RE.

### Gap #20 — Manufacturers: ABL vs. PO+AR, Inventory+AR+Equipment as Combined Collateral

**Strong calls:** Acela (Call 5) — extremely detailed on why PO financing fails for custom manufacturers; deposit-cycle collapse; GAAP installment accounting mismatch; bridge-then-ABL sequencing.
**Weak calls:** All others (no manufacturing content).

**Key patterns from Acela:**
- PO financing does NOT work for custom manufacturers because inputs come from hundreds of vendors (not a single supplier relationship), every unit is unique, and the PO cannot be cleanly assigned.
- The working capital gap is caused by the mismatch between: (a) 9-month production cycle, (b) customers no longer paying deposits, (c) vendors requiring 30–60–90 day payment.
- GAAP installment accounting makes cash-to-income reconciliation impossible — a major underwriting challenge for bank-statement-based lenders.
- ABL (asset-based lending) is the right permanent structure for a manufacturer — it can borrow against WIP, finished inventory, AR, and equipment. But it takes 6–8 weeks to set up.
- Bridge (revenue-based, fast) = step 1 to stabilize while ABL is assembled.
- Municipal client concentration (85–90%) = deposit risk (city councils stopped paying deposits) but also = extremely low credit risk on AR (government obligors).
- Mike's strategic framing: the company is "sold out through 2026, selling 2027" — the business quality is not in question, only the liquidity gap from deposit-cycle failure.
